The Complete History of Troy, Michigan: From Founding to Today

Discover 200 years of Troy history, from frontier farmland through post-WWII suburban explosion to premier business and residential community.

Troy's transformation from sparsely populated farmland to Michigan's most affluent city represents the quintessential American suburban success story. In just seven decades, Troy evolved from rural township to thriving city of over 87,000, home to major corporate headquarters, excellent schools, and diverse, prosperous communities.

As Troy's local roofing experts since 2009, Elite Roofing is proud to serve this dynamic community. We've worked throughout Troy's neighborhoods, from established subdivisions to newer developments, protecting homes in Oakland County's premier city.

Let's explore the remarkable transformation of Troy from farmland to suburban powerhouse.

Early Settlement & Farming Era (1820s-1950)

1822 - Troy Township Established

Troy Township was organized as one of Oakland County's first townships, named after Troy, New York, following the common practice of naming Michigan settlements after Eastern cities.

The first European-American settlers arrived in the 1820s, finding rolling terrain, scattered oak groves, and fertile soil. The land was previously inhabited by various Native American peoples, including the Potawatomi and Ojibwe, who were displaced by treaties opening Southeast Michigan to American settlement.

Early Troy was entirely agricultural. Farmers grew wheat, corn, and hay, raised livestock, and sold produce to Detroit's growing markets. Small settlements emerged around crossroads and mills, but Troy remained rural and sparsely populated throughout the 19th century.

1827 - First School Established

Troy's commitment to education began early with the establishment of its first school. This emphasis on education would later become a defining characteristic of the suburban city.

By 1900, Troy Township had only about 1,400 residents scattered across family farms. Life changed little through the early 20th century. While Detroit boomed with automotive manufacturing, Troy remained peaceful farmland‚Äîa "day's buggy ride" from the city.

Even by 1950, Troy's population was only 6,300, primarily farmers and rural residents. The township seemed destined to remain agricultural indefinitely. But forces were gathering that would transform Troy beyond recognition in just one generation.

Suburban Explosion (1950-1980)

1955 - City of Troy Incorporated

Troy incorporated as a city to control its own development as suburban growth accelerated. This decision proved crucial in shaping Troy's high-quality development standards.

Post-World War II prosperity, the baby boom, and automotive industry success drove massive suburban expansion. Detroit-area families sought new homes with yards, good schools, and modern amenities. Troy's location‚Äînorth of Detroit with excellent highway access‚Äîmade it perfect for suburban development.

Farmland was sold and subdivided at unprecedented rates. Where corn grew one year, ranch homes appeared the next. Developers built entire neighborhoods in months. Young families moved in, establishing communities virtually overnight.

Population Explosion

Troy's growth was staggering: 6,300 residents in 1950 to 19,200 in 1960 to 39,400 in 1970 to 67,100 by 1980. The city more than doubled every decade!

Infrastructure Development

Troy built schools, parks, libraries, and municipal facilities at breakneck pace. The city's planning department worked to maintain quality despite rapid growth.

1969 - Oakland Mall Opens

The opening of Oakland Mall symbolized Troy's transformation from farmland to suburban commercial center. The mall became a regional shopping destination and economic anchor.

Troy's school district became a major draw for families. New schools opened regularly, and the district developed a reputation for academic excellence that attracted residents willing to pay premium prices for Troy homes.

By 1980, Troy had transformed completely. Virtually all farmland had been developed. The city offered excellent housing, top schools, shopping, and services‚Äîeverything suburban families desired. Troy had become Oakland County's premiere residential address.

Corporate Headquarters Era (1980-2000)

As Troy matured residentially, it began attracting corporate headquarters and office development, transforming from bedroom community to major employment center.

1980s - Big Beaver Road Corporate Corridor

Big Beaver Road (M-59) developed into a major corporate corridor. Office buildings and corporate campuses rose along the highway, bringing thousands of white-collar jobs to Troy.

Major corporations established headquarters in Troy, attracted by educated workforce, excellent location, quality of life, and business-friendly environment. Kmart, Merrill Lynch, Kelly Services, and other major companies built significant operations in Troy.

The Somerset Collection, an upscale shopping mall, opened in 1996, establishing Troy as a luxury retail destination. The mall's success reflected Troy's affluence and attracted more high-end retailers and restaurants.

Economic Diversification

Troy successfully balanced residential quality with commercial development. Unlike pure bedroom suburbs, Troy offered substantial employment, reducing commute times and strengthening the local economy.

International Businesses

Troy attracted international corporations, particularly Japanese automotive suppliers supporting Detroit's auto industry. This international presence added cultural diversity and global connections.

By 2000, Troy had achieved remarkable success: population of 80,959, median household income well above state averages, excellent schools ranked among Michigan's best, low crime, and a thriving business community. Troy represented suburban success‚Äîthe American Dream realized.

Modern Troy (2000-Today)

The 21st century brought new challenges and continued evolution as Troy matured from growing suburb to established city.

2008-2009 - Economic Recession Impact

The automotive industry crisis and recession affected Troy as corporate relocations slowed and some headquarters downsized. However, Troy's economic diversity cushioned the impact compared to manufacturing-dependent cities.

Troy adapted by focusing on emerging sectors: technology, healthcare, professional services, and international business. The city attracted tech companies and startups, leveraging its educated workforce and quality of life to compete for knowledge economy jobs.

Growing Diversity

Troy became increasingly diverse, with significant Asian (particularly Indian and Chinese), Middle Eastern (particularly Chaldean), and other immigrant populations. This diversity enriched the community and created international business connections.

Urban Amenities

Troy developed urban amenities rare in suburbs: diverse dining (reflecting international populations), cultural events, libraries, and recreation facilities that created city-like vibrancy.

Education remained Troy's calling card. Troy School District continued ranking among Michigan's top districts, and Troy High School consistently achieved recognition as one of America's best public high schools. Families continued choosing Troy primarily for schools.

2020s - Continued Evolution

Troy entered the 2020s with population around 87,000, stable and prosperous. The city focused on maintaining infrastructure, adapting to remote work trends, and preserving quality of life that made Troy special.

Today's Troy balances multiple identities: premier residential suburb, major employment center, culturally diverse community, and shopping destination. The transformation from farmland to modern city took just seven decades‚Äîa remarkably rapid evolution that created one of Michigan's most successful communities.

Historical Landmarks & Sites

While Troy's history is relatively recent, several sites and landmarks connect to its past:

Troy Historic Village

Location: 60 W Wattles Rd
This living history museum preserves Troy's 19th-century heritage with historic buildings relocated and restored, including one-room schoolhouse, general store, log cabin, and church. Programs and events bring Troy's farming era to life.

Troy Public Library

Location: 510 W Big Beaver Rd
Troy's commitment to education extends to its excellent library system. The main library serves as community hub and reflects Troy's values of learning and literacy.

Somerset Collection

Location: 2800 W Big Beaver Rd
This upscale shopping destination symbolizes Troy's affluence and transformation. The luxury mall attracts visitors region-wide and anchors Troy's retail sector.

Troy Community Center

Location: 3179 Livernois Rd
This modern recreation facility reflects Troy's investment in quality of life amenities. The center offers fitness, programs, and community gathering space.

Troy Nature Center

Location: 6685 Coolidge Hwy
Preserving natural areas within suburban development, the Nature Center provides environmental education and connects residents to Troy's pre-development landscape.

Veterans Memorial

Location: Civic Center Dr
Troy honors military service with memorials and monuments, recognizing veterans from all conflicts and maintaining connection to American history.

Business Leaders

Educational Leaders

Troy's Residential Architecture

Troy's housing stock reflects suburban development eras from the 1950s through today. Ranch homes from the 1950s-60s, split-levels and colonials from the 1970s, and larger homes from the 1980s-90s create diverse neighborhoods.

Unlike older Michigan cities with historic Victorian homes, Troy's character comes from quality post-war construction. These homes feature solid construction, good materials, and thoughtful design that has aged well. Established neighborhoods with mature trees and landscaping create attractive, livable communities.
